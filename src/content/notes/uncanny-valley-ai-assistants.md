AI assistants are crossing a threshold. They're no longer just tools that respond to commands — they're starting to anticipate needs, suggest actions, and hold conversations that feel almost human. And that "almost" is the problem.

The uncanny valley isn't just for robots with faces. It applies to any system that gets close enough to human behavior that its shortcomings become unsettling. When an AI suggests something slightly off, it doesn't feel like a machine making a reasonable mistake — it feels like something human acting strange.

I've been thinking about this a lot while working on Copilot. The design decisions around how much personality to show, when to proactively suggest vs. wait for a prompt, how to signal confidence — these are all uncanny valley problems.

The solution isn't to make AI more human. It's to make the boundary between machine capability and human expectation more legible. People need to understand what they're interacting with, not be tricked into thinking it's something it's not.

The best AI experiences I've used don't try to hide what they are. They're competent, clear about their limits, and comfortable being tools rather than companions.
