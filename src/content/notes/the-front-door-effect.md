The first thing people see shapes everything they believe about what's inside. This is as true for products as it is for buildings — and most teams underestimate it.

![The first thing people see shapes everything they believe](/images/placeholder-observations.svg)

## The original front door

Architects have known this forever. The entrance of a building isn't just a door — it's a statement about what the building values, who it's for, and how it wants you to feel.

A courthouse with massive columns says "this is serious and permanent." A café with an open patio says "come in, stay a while." A hospital with clear wayfinding says "we know you're stressed, let us help."

The front door is never neutral.

## The digital front door

In products, the front door is your:

- **Onboarding flow** — does it feel like a welcome or a tax form?
- **First screen** — does it show what matters or everything at once?
- **Empty state** — does it guide or abandon?
- **Loading state** — does it respect the user's time?

Every one of these is a design decision. Most teams treat them as afterthoughts.

> The front door isn't the lobby where people wait. It's the entire first experience.

## Why it matters more now

Attention is scarcer than ever. People don't read manuals. They don't watch tutorials. They open your product, form an impression in seconds, and decide whether to stay.

The front door is where trust starts or ends. Design it accordingly.

## Three practical rules

1. **Design the first screen like it's a poster.** If it needs explanation, it's too complex.
2. **Show value before asking for commitment.** Let people experience the product before demanding signup, permissions, or payment.
3. **Make the empty state feel intentional.** An empty screen isn't a bug — it's the most important moment in the product.
