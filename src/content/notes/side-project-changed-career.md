Nobody asked me to build that little tool. It wasn't on any roadmap. It wasn't going to get me promoted. But it's the thing that eventually changed the trajectory of my career more than any OKR or performance review.

Side projects get a bad reputation in corporate environments. They're seen as distractions, evidence that you're not "focused" or "aligned." But in my experience, the opposite is true. Side projects are where you develop the skills and perspectives that make you valuable in your day job — you just can't always predict which ones or when.

The project I'm talking about was simple: a small internal tool that automated a tedious design handoff process. I built it over a few weekends because I was frustrated, not because I had a strategy. But that tool ended up being adopted by three other teams, led to a presentation at our org all-hands, and gave me a reputation as someone who could see beyond the Figma file.

The lesson wasn't "build tools." It was that the space between "what I'm supposed to do" and "what I'm curious about" is where the most interesting work lives. The career moves that matter rarely come from following the path that's already laid out. They come from the detours.
