Every designer I know struggles with the same thing: knowing when to stop refining and start shipping.

It's not because we don't care about quality. It's the opposite — we care so much that the gap between what we imagine and what we can build in a reasonable timeframe feels unbearable. So we iterate. And iterate. And somehow the thing that was supposed to launch in March becomes a Q3 project.

Here's what I've learned the hard way: shipping is itself a feature. A product that exists, even imperfectly, creates feedback loops that no amount of internal review can replicate. Users don't experience your design in a Figma file — they experience it in the context of their actual work, their actual day, their actual frustrations.

The best teams I've worked on had a shared language for "good enough to learn from." Not "good enough to be proud of" — that's a different bar. But good enough that putting it in front of real people will teach you something you couldn't learn from another internal review.

This doesn't mean shipping garbage. It means being honest about what you're optimizing for: the quality of the artifact, or the quality of your learning.
